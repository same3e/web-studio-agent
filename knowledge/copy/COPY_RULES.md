## Migrated legacy copy rules

# Copy rules

- Lead with a concrete user benefit, offer, audience, or proof.
- Make headings scannable and CTAs specific to the next action.
- Prefer facts, outcomes, process, and evidence over generic claims.
- Avoid empty phrases such as "team of professionals".
- Match language, tone, claims, and compliance needs to the client and market.
- Follow the mandatory hero hierarchy in `DESIGN_RULES.md`: do not write a small label or pre-heading above H1 without explicit user approval.

## Copy mode

Use `conversion-first` by default for services, clinics, construction, manufacturing, delivery, marketing, local businesses, and lead-generation landing pages. Use `brand-led` only when the user explicitly asks for image-led communication, or for a portfolio, fashion, art, architecture, or editorial project. Even in `brand-led`, the offer must remain understandable.

## Conversion-first copy

The hero must answer: what is offered, for whom, what result the client receives, what differentiates the offer, and what happens after the CTA. Distribute the answers across H1, supporting copy, CTA, and proof points.

Use this hierarchy:

1. H1 — a concrete offer or client result.
2. Supporting copy — how it works, what is included, or how uncertainty is reduced.
3. Primary CTA — a specific action and expected result.
4. One to three nearby proof points — verified facts only and only when they add value.
5. Optional risk reducer — only a real condition such as a free visit or fixed price.

### H1

The entire conversion-first hero — H1, supporting copy, CTA, and proof points together — must communicate at least two of: service, client result, object, timeframe, financial condition, geography, or a clear first step. The H1 itself must clearly communicate at least the service, offer, or client result. Do not overload an H1 merely to satisfy a formula; write naturally for the niche.

Do not use an abstract phrase as the main H1 without concrete continuation: “new level”, “care built on”, “space of possibilities”, “quality you can trust”, “technology of the future”, “individual approach”, “making dreams real”, “your confidence”, “harmony and comfort”, “precision in every detail”, “the art of creating”, “more than”, “a place where”, “a modern approach to”, or “solutions for your future”. An abstract line may be a secondary slogan only after the offer is clear.

### Supporting copy and CTA

Supporting copy must add information rather than restate the H1: scope, process, inputs, first-step output, or risk reduction.

Write CTA labels around the outcome: `Get a cost estimate`, `Select an option for my site`, `Book a diagnostic visit`, `Get a treatment plan`, `See a lead forecast`, `Calculate delivery`, or `Discuss the task with a designer`. Avoid `Learn more`, `Details`, `Submit`, `Start`, `Go`, and `Leave a request` unless no precise action is possible.

### Proof and factual reliability

Use one to three verifiable proof points only where they add value: experience, project volume, real timeline, guarantee, review or rating, production, process, qualification, technology, fixed price, contract, or service composition. Do not add proof cards just to fill space. Never invent figures, prices, guarantees, ratings, certificates, availability, technologies, or advantages. If confirmed facts are missing, ask the client instead of replacing them with generic praise.

Placeholders are allowed only in internal documentation. Never put an unverified placeholder, test figure, or invented claim in a production interface.

## Anti-template rule

Do not mechanically repeat constructions such as “We’ll select X and calculate Y in Z minutes” across niches. Examples show commercial logic, not offer templates. Adapt the structure to the niche, audience, client decision, and verified facts. Use different approaches where they fit: result-led, problem-led, process-led, selection-led, diagnostic, and proof-led. Neighbouring projects must not receive near-identical headlines with only nouns replaced.

## Regulated and medical claims

For clinics and regulated services, do not guarantee a treatment result; promise painlessness without a confirmed basis; use unproven superlatives such as “best doctors” or “safest treatment”; present a preliminary cost as final; or conflate consultation, diagnostics, a preliminary plan, and a final medical decision. Copy must match confirmed client materials and the requirements of the specific market.

## Multilingual copy

Do not translate commercial copy literally. Preserve the offer, facts, and CTA intent, but rewrite naturally for each language. Account for differing Russian, English, and Georgian text lengths; adapt layout rather than cutting commercial meaning to force equal height. Keep factual claims identical across every language version.

### Hero Copy Score

Score every conversion-first hero from 0 to 10:

- clarity of offer: 0–2;
- specificity of outcome: 0–2;
- differentiation or proof: 0–2;
- CTA clarity: 0–2;
- factual reliability: 0–2.

Before approval, check that the offer is understood without the rest of the site, the first-contact result is clear, the CTA matches that result, proof is present when verified facts add value, and no claim is invented. If a competitor could replace the company name without changing the meaning, check for at least one business-specific fact, process element, audience condition, or proof. Do not invent artificial uniqueness when the client has not confirmed it. Require at least 8/10 for `conversion-first`. A lower score requires a rewrite unless the user explicitly chooses `brand-led`.
